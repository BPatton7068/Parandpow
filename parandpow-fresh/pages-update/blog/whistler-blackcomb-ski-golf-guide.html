Whistler Blackcomb: The Ultimate Ski & Golf Guide

By Par & Pow Jan 16, 2026 15 min read 🇨🇦 British Columbia, Canada

I've been to Whistler more times than I can count, and it's the one destination I recommend without hesitation to anyone asking about combining skiing and golf. Not because it's the closest or cheapest — it's neither — but because it does both at a genuinely world-class level without compromise. Most "dual sport" destinations ask you to accept mediocre golf or mediocre skiing. Whistler asks you to accept neither.

Here's everything you need to plan the trip properly, based on having actually done it.

The short version: Ski November through April. Golf May through October. Or — and this is the real move — go in late April or early October when you can sometimes do both in the same trip, conditions permitting.

THE MOUNTAIN

Whistler Blackcomb is two mountains connected by the Peak 2 Peak Gondola — the longest and highest lift of its kind in the world. Together they offer 8,171 acres of terrain, 200+ marked trails, and an average snowfall of 469 inches. The terrain split is genuinely diverse: from wide groomed boulevards to steep couloirs to some of the best in-bounds tree skiing in North America.

What kind of rider is Whistler for?

Everyone, honestly. But it earns its reputation most convincingly with intermediate to advanced riders. If you're a strong intermediate, Whistler will challenge and excite you for a week without sending you somewhere you're not ready for. If you're advanced, the backcountry access, steeps off the Couloir Extreme, and terrain on the Blackcomb Glacier will keep you busy for as long as you're willing to stay.

Don't miss

  • Peak 2 Peak Gondola — even if you don't ski it, ride it. The views of both mountains and the valley below are unlike anything else in North America.
  • Spanky's Ladder on Blackcomb — a hike-to zone that opens up some of the most rewarding expert terrain on the mountain.
  • The Symphony Amphitheatre on Whistler — wide open, usually less crowded, incredible for carving on a bluebird day.
  • 7th Heaven on Blackcomb — the top of the mountain zone, consistently good snow, stunning exposure.

⛷️ Fast Facts — Whistler Blackcomb

  • 8,171 skiable acres across 2 mountains
  • 200+ marked trails (20% beginner / 55% intermediate / 25% expert)
  • Average annual snowfall: 469 inches
  • Peak 2 Peak Gondola: 4.4km span, 436m above the valley
  • Season: typically November through May/June (glacier skiing)
  • Lift tickets: CAD $230–280/day. Buy in advance online — always cheaper.
  • Stay in: Whistler Village (ski-in/ski-out), Upper Village, or Creekside

THE GOLF

Whistler has four golf courses and all of them are worth your time. But two stand above the rest.

Nicklaus North Golf Course

This is the one. Designed by Jack Nicklaus and opened in 1996, Nicklaus North sits on the shore of Green Lake with views of Whistler and Blackcomb rising behind it. On a clear day it might be the most scenic golf setting in North America. The course plays to 6,961 yards from the tips and has enough teeth to keep a scratch golfer engaged, but it's also walkable and enjoyable for mid-handicappers who can manage a course with water on nearly every hole.

Book tee times directly at nicklasnorth.com. Morning rounds sell out weeks in advance in peak summer. Book as early as possible.

Whistler Golf Club

Arnold Palmer design, opened in 1983. More forgiving than Nicklaus North, but no less beautiful — it runs through old-growth forest with the mountains as a constant backdrop. This is the right course for a group with mixed handicaps. Greens are excellent and the pace of play is generally well-managed.

Fairmont Chateau Whistler Golf Club

Robert Trent Jones Jr. design. Hilly, challenging, and offers some of the most dramatic elevation changes you'll find at a resort course. Not the place to go for a casual round — it'll test your game and your legs. Worth it once.

HOW TO STACK THE TRIP

Option 1: The Pure Snow Trip (Nov–April)

Come for the skiing. Full stop. Don't try to golf — the courses are closed. Stay in the Village, ski hard for 4–5 days, eat well, recover in the hot tub. This is the trip most people do and it's excellent.

Option 2: The Pure Golf Trip (May–October)

The mountain is green, the courses are open, and Whistler Village is genuinely lovely in summer. Play Nicklaus North and Whistler Golf Club back to back. Add a round at Chateau Whistler if you want a challenge. Hike, bike, eat at Araxi. This is an underrated summer trip.

Option 3: The Shoulder Season Double (Late April or Early October)

This is the move for dual-sport addicts. In late April, the mountain is still open (often with excellent spring conditions on the upper mountain) and Nicklaus North typically opens in mid-April. You can legitimately ski in the morning and golf in the afternoon on the same day. The light is incredible, crowds are down, and prices drop significantly off peak.

The Editor's pick: Late April. Spring snow on the upper mountain, Nicklaus North freshly open, shoulder season pricing, and the village is quiet enough to actually get a table at dinner without a reservation two weeks out.

WHERE TO STAY

Ski-in/ski-out: Fairmont Chateau Whistler (Upper Village) or Four Seasons Resort Whistler. Both are genuinely excellent and worth the premium if budget allows.

Mid-range: Whistler Village Inn + Suites or Delta Hotels by Marriott. Both are well-located in the Village, steps from the gondolas.

Best value: Creekside — quieter, slightly cheaper, and the Creekside gondola gets you on Whistler Mountain without the Village crowds.

GEAR NOTES FOR THIS TRIP

Whistler weather is famously variable. Pack for rain even in peak winter — the Village sits at 675m and gets rain events that become powder 500m higher up. A quality waterproof jacket isn't optional here.

GETTING THERE

  • Fly into: Vancouver International (YVR) — 2 hour drive to Whistler on the Sea-to-Sky Highway
  • Car rental vs. shuttle: If you're skiing only, take the Epic Rides shuttle from YVR (no parking hassle). If you're golfing or want flexibility, rent a car.
  • Drive time: 1h45m–2h30m depending on traffic and season. The Sea-to-Sky is one of the most spectacular drives in North America — budget time to stop at Shannon Falls and the Sea-to-Sky Gondola in Squamish.

BUDGET PLANNING

  • Lift tickets: CAD $230–280/day advance purchase. Epic or Ikon passes offer significant savings if you ski multiple resorts per season.
  • Golf: Nicklaus North CAD $180–220/round. Whistler Golf Club CAD $120–160/round.
  • Accommodation: CAD $300–600/night depending on property and season.
  • Food: Budget CAD $80–120/person/day for meals and drinks in the Village.
  • Total 5-day trip estimate (skiing): CAD $3,500–6,000 per person depending on accommodation and spending level.

Pro tip: Book accommodation through the Whistler Blackcomb website directly — they often bundle lift tickets with lodging at a meaningful discount, especially for multi-night stays.

THE BOTTOM LINE

Whistler is the benchmark. Every other dual-sport destination I compare against it. The skiing is legitimately world-class. The golf at Nicklaus North is as good as any resort course in North America. The village infrastructure — restaurants, bars, hotels — is polished without being sterile. And the Sea-to-Sky approach makes the journey part of the experience.

If you're only doing one dual-sport trip in your lifetime, make it Whistler.
